## Question 2
### How can you use B = MAP to make making new habits easier? What are M, A and P.

**B = MAP** is BJ Fogg’s behavior model. It explains that a new habit forms when three elements align:
- **M (Motivation):** Your desire or reason to do the habit.
- **A (Ability):** How easy it is to perform the habit.
- **P (Prompt):** A trigger or reminder that tells you to act.

### How It Helps
- **Reduce reliance on motivation:** Motivation is unreliable; instead, make habits tiny and easy.
- **Increase ability:** Simplify the habit so it takes little effort (e.g., flossing one tooth).
- **Use prompts:** Tie the habit to an existing routine (e.g., brushing teeth → floss one tooth).

### Example
- Want to start exercising?  
  - **Motivation:** You want better health.  
  - **Ability:** Start with 2 push-ups (easy).  
  - **Prompt:** Do it right after brushing your teeth.

By combining these three, the habit becomes natural and sustainable.

## Question 3
### Why is it important to "Shine" or Celebrate after each successful completion of a habit? (This is the most important concept in today's topic. Whatever you celebrate becomes a habit)
Celebration is the secret ingredient that makes habits stick. When you **shine** or celebrate after completing a habit, you create positive emotions that wire the behavior into your brain.

### Why It Matters
- **Emotion creates memory:** The brain remembers habits that feel good.
- **Motivation boost:** Small celebrations make you want to repeat the habit.
- **Identity shift:** Each celebration reinforces "I am the kind of person who does this."

### How to Celebrate
- Smile, fist pump, or say "Yes!" out loud.
- Do a small happy dance.
- Simply acknowledge: "I’m proud of myself."

## Question 6
### Write about the book's perspective on how to make a habit easier to do.
James Clear suggests using the **Law of Least Effort**:  
- **Reduce friction:** Make the habit simple and convenient.  
- **Environment design:** Place cues where you’ll see them (e.g., keep fruit on the counter).  
- **Two-minute rule:** Start habits small — if it takes less than 2 minutes, it’s easy to begin.  
- **Automation:** Use tools or systems that remove effort (e.g., automatic savings transfers).

## Question 7
### Write about the book's perspective on how to make a habit harder to do.
To break bad habits, apply the opposite principle — **increase friction**:  
- **Remove triggers:** Keep temptations out of sight (e.g., don’t keep junk food at home).  
- **Add steps:** Make the habit less convenient (e.g., uninstall distracting apps).  
- **Use commitment devices:** Lock yourself into better choices (e.g., give your friend money that you lose if you skip the gym).  
- **Change environment:** Design surroundings so the bad habit is harder to access.

## Question 8
### Pick one habit that you would like to do more of. What are the steps that you can take to make the cue obvious or the habit more attractive or easy and or response satisfying?
I’d like to **read 10 minutes every day** to grow my knowledge and relax.

Steps to Make It Stick
### 1. Make the Cue Obvious
- Place the book on my pillow so I see it before bed.
- Set a reminder on my phone at 9 PM.

### 2. Make the Habit Attractive
- Choose books I’m genuinely curious about.
- Pair reading with a cup of tea to make it enjoyable.

### 3. Make the Habit Easy
- Start small: commit to just 2 pages.
- Keep the book within reach (bedside table).

### 4. Make the Response Satisfying
- Celebrate finishing by saying “Nice work!” or smiling.
- Track progress with a habit tracker to see streaks grow.
- Reward myself with a short break or favorite snack after reading.

## Question 9
### Pick one habit that you would like to eliminate or do less of? What are the steps that you can take to make it make the cue invisible or the process unattractive or hard or the response unsatisfying?
I’d like to **spend less time on social media** because it distracts me and reduces productivity.

Steps to Reduce It
### 1. Make the Cue Invisible
- Remove apps from the home screen.
- Turn off notifications so I don’t get constant prompts.

### 2. Make the Habit Unattractive
- Remind myself of the downsides (lost time, stress).
- Replace scrolling with a more rewarding activity (reading or walking).

### 3. Make the Habit Hard
- Log out of accounts after each use.
- Use website blockers or set time limits.
- Keep the phone away during focused work.

### 4. Make the Response Unsatisfying
- Track wasted time to see the negative impact.
- Share goals with a friend who holds me accountable.
- Attach a small penalty (e.g., donate money if I exceed screen time).
